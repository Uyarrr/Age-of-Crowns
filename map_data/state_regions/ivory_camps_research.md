# Ivory Camp Distribution Research

## Scope and balance

- Previous map: no `building_ivory_camp` state-region caps.
- Rebuilt map: 86 ivory-bearing states and 371 total camp levels.
- Placement uses `capped_resources`, not `arable_resources`, so ivory hunting is wildlife-limited and cannot consume an entire state's farm capacity.
- No discoverable `resource` block is used. Elephant ranges and ivory-working regions are known at the 1444 start, even if exploitation can expand later through construction and technology.

Each level outputs 20 ivory at the first production method, so the total is intentionally conservative. Ivory is currently only produced by the camp PMs; it has no broad production-method sink in the local files yet.

## Tier rules

| Levels | Role | Examples |
| ---: | --- | --- |
| 8-10 | Core elephant and ivory zones | Congo Basin, Gabon, Equateur, Tanganyika, Equatoria, Kasai |
| 5-7 | Major regional zones | South Cameroon, Uganda, Kenya, Mozambique-Zambezi, Assam, Myanmar, Cambodia-Laos |
| 3-4 | Local or trade-linked zone | Ivory Coast, Benin, Gold Coast, Ethiopia, Malaya, Sumatra, Yunnan |
| 2 | Marginal but plausible range edge | Sahel-edge states, East Bengal, Bihar, Orissa, Guangxi, Borneo interiors |

## Geographic anchors

- West Africa: Upper Guinea, Ivory Coast, Gold Coast, Benin, Yoruba, Niger Delta, and Nigeria receive modest camps for forest/savanna elephant range and 15th-16th century Afro-Portuguese ivory trade.
- Central Africa: Gabon, South Cameroon, Ubangi-Shari, Congo, Equateur, Congo Orientale, Kasai, Bas-Congo, and Angola are the highest-cap area because forest elephants and riverine trade networks make the Congo Basin the strongest historical source region.
- East Africa: Kenya, Rift Valley, Tanganyika, Equatoria, Uganda, Mozambique, Zambezi, and Zambezia cover savanna elephants and Swahili/Indian Ocean ivory routes.
- Ethiopia and Sudan: Oromia, Gonder, Amhara, Blue Nile, Kordofan, Darfur, Waddai, and Chad get small caps for range-edge and caravan access. This keeps Ethiopia from being blank without turning it into a dominant ivory producer.
- Southern Africa: Botswana, Zambia, Zambezi, Hereroland, Zululand, Transvaal, and Lourenco Marques get small to medium caps for savanna elephant range and southern trade routes.
- South Asia: Assam, Ceylon, Mysore, Central Provinces, Travancore, Madras, and smaller eastern Indian caps represent Asian elephant range and older elephant capture/use traditions. Asian ivory is kept below African output because tusking rates and export scale are lower.
- Southeast Asia: Myanmar, Cambodia, Laos, Siam, Malaya, Sumatra, Borneo, Yunnan, and Guangxi receive modest caps for Asian elephant range. Java, the Philippines, Japan, Korea, and most China states are excluded.
- North Africa, Arabia, Europe, the Americas, Madagascar, and Oceania receive no ivory camps.

## Method

1. Start from elephant habitat and historical range, not modern wildlife populations alone.
2. Weight by 1444 accessibility: river basins, forest-savanna margins, caravan routes, Swahili coast trade, and Portuguese-era West African ivory centers matter.
3. Keep African forest and savanna elephant zones stronger than Asian elephant zones.
4. Give minor caps to plausible regional producers so gameplay is not locked to one colonial corridor.
5. Avoid overproducing a luxury wildlife good until the economy has more ivory demand.

## Principal sources

- IUCN SSC African Elephant Specialist Group, African Elephant Status Reports and African Elephant Database range material.
- IUCN Red List assessments for African savanna elephant, African forest elephant, and Asian elephant.
- WWF and African elephant range summaries for savanna, forest, and Central/West African habitat.
- Metropolitan Museum of Art and related Afro-Portuguese ivory scholarship for Sierra Leone, Benin, Kongo, and 15th-16th century ivory-working/export context.
- Historical scholarship on Swahili, West African, and Indian Ocean ivory trade routes.
